# AyushBot Brand Guide

## 1. Brand Essence

### 1.1 Brand One-Liner

**AyushBot is the calm, evidence-grounded co-pilot that helps ASHA workers make safer decisions for every family they serve.**

### 1.2 Brand Promise

To turn cutting-edge AI and IoT into a trustworthy, human tool that fits seamlessly into the realities of village health work — offline, multilingual, low-friction — without ever overshadowing the ASHA’s judgment.

### 1.3 Brand Personality

If AyushBot were a person, it would be:

- **Calm** — Steady under pressure, never panicked.
- **Supportive** — Speaks to the ASHA as a partner, not a supervisor.
- **Precise** — Every recommendation is grounded in guidelines and data.
- **Humble** — Admits uncertainty and points to sources instead of pretending to know everything.
- **Optimistic** — Focused on what can be done now to help.

Three words to remember: **Calm · Competent · Caring**.

***

## 2. Brand Positioning

### 2.1 Category

- Digital clinical decision support for frontline health workers.
- AI + IoT for rural primary care and maternal–child health.

### 2.2 Target Users & Stakeholders

- **Primary:** ASHA workers in India.
- **Secondary:** PHC medical officers, ANMs, and nurses.
- **Tertiary:** District health officials, policymakers, donors, and researchers.

### 2.3 Differentiation

AyushBot is not just "an app"; it is a **field-proven workflow companion** that:

- Works offline at the edge.
- Integrates directly with low-cost sensors.
- Learns locally via federated learning.
- Speaks local languages and cites Indian guidelines.

Messaging angle: "Global-grade AI, built for the last mile."

## 3. Verbal Identity

### 3.1 Voice & Tone

**Voice:** Clear, plain, confident.

**Tone by scenario:**

- **Routine triage:** Neutral to warm. "Let’s check a few things together."
- **Medium risk:** Serious, focused but not alarming. "The child needs extra attention. Here’s what you can do."
- **High / critical risk:** Direct, unambiguous. "This is an emergency. Refer the child to the nearest facility now."
- **Uncertainty:** Honest. "The information is not enough for a confident answer. Please follow the standard referral protocol."

Avoid:

- Jargon-heavy explanations.
- Overly casual or jokey language.
- Blameful phrasing ("You forgot to...").

Prefer:

- Short sentences.
- Active voice.
- Concrete verbs ("Give", "Refer", "Check", "Call").

### 3.2 UX Writing Principles

- Always start with **what to do**, then explain **why**.
- Use the ASHA’s point of view: "You can…" instead of "The app will…".
- For numerical values, always provide context: "SpO₂ is 89% (this is low)".
- For instructions, use ordered lists for steps and bullets for options.

## 4. Visual Identity

### 4.1 Logo

**Concept:**

- A stylized heart or shield merged with a subtle waveform and a village skyline / hut outline.
- Forms an abstract "A" for AyushBot.

**Usage:**

- Primary logo: Icon + wordmark "AyushBot".
- Wordmark: Set in Noto Sans with slight custom modifications (e.g., rounded "A" crossbar) to be unique but still system-friendly.

**Clear space:**

- Minimum padding: height of the uppercase "A" on all sides.

**Minimum size:**

- 24 dp icon on Android; for print, 15 mm width.

### 4.2 Color Palette

AyushBot’s color palette mirrors the app’s design specs but is defined at brand level.

**Core brand colors:**

- Ayush Teal: `#006874` (primary).
- River Slate: `#4A6267` (secondary).
- Saffron Warmth: `#7D5700` (accent).

**Secondary support colors:**

- Forest Green: `#1B6E2C`.
- Sky Mist: `#97F0FF`.
- Clay Neutral: `#E2D7CE` (optional printed material background).

**Usage ratios:**

- 60% neutral (white/near-white, light greys).
- 25% primary teal.
- 10% secondary slate.
- 5% accent saffron and state colors.

### 4.3 Imagery & Illustration

Where imagery is used (training materials, website, pitch decks):

- Use **illustrations** more than stock photography.
- Show ASHAs in realistic attire and settings, not idealised hospital environments.
- Portray diversity in region, skin tone, and environment.
- Avoid imagery of suffering children as a primary visual; focus on care and agency.

## 5. Application of Brand in Product

### 5.1 App Icon

- Background: Ayush Teal.
- Foreground: White logo icon (heart/shield + waveform + hut/A).
- Simple silhouette, no fine details that disappear at 48×48 px.

### 5.2 Launch Screen

- Plain background in Ayush Teal.
- Centered logo and wordmark in white.
- Optional tagline: "Safer decisions, together." underneath in Title Small.

Keep launch screen simple; loading indicators should be subtle and not used as an ad space.

### 5.3 In-App Branding

- Top app bar in neutral surface color; avoid always-on primary bars.
- Use teal primarily for actions and highlights (buttons, active tabs), not for large backgrounds.
- Reserve red only for errors and critical clinical states.

***

## 6. Brand Architecture & Naming

### 6.1 Product & Module Names

- **AyushBot App** — Android app for ASHAs.
- **AyushBot Gateway** — Edge server at PHC.
- **AyushBot Sense** — IoT sensor pack.
- **AyushBot Insight** — Analytics dashboard for officials.

Naming should be short, pronounceable, and consistent.

### 6.2 Feature Naming Guidelines

- Names should reflect intent, not technology.
- Good: "Safe Referral", "Offline Visit", "Case History".
- Avoid: "Edge Sync", "RAG Query", "Federated Round" (backend-only terms).

***

## 7. Brand Principles for Decision-Making

When making trade-offs as PM/CEO, apply these brand principles:

1. **Safety over speed.** If a UI pattern is faster but increases risk of misinterpretation in a critical flow, choose the safer pattern.
2. **Clarity over cleverness.** A simple, slightly boring screen that ASHAs use correctly is better than a clever design that confuses them.
3. **Evidence over aesthetics alone.** Color, copy, and interaction changes should ideally be measured (SUS, task completion, error rate), not just designed.
4. **Local relevance over global trends.** It is more important that the design feels native to an ASHA’s context than that it looks like the latest Silicon Valley app.
5. **Human agency over AI showmanship.** AyushBot must always frame itself as assisting the ASHA, not replacing her. Avoid UI patterns that imply fully autonomous decisions.

***

## 8. Brand in External Communications

### 8.1 Pitch Decks

- Use the same color palette and typography as the app.
- Lead with **stories of ASHAs and families helped**, then show the tech.
- Always connect metrics (reduced unnecessary referrals, improved protocol adherence) to human outcomes.

### 8.2 Website & Social Media

- Tone should match in-app voice: clear, calm, grounded.
- Avoid fear-based messaging; emphasise empowerment and support.
- Highlight partnerships with government / NGOs to signal legitimacy.

### 8.3 Documentation & Training

- Use brand colors and logo sparingly.
- Priority: legible diagrams, step-by-step screenshots, and localized language.

***

## 9. Governance

### 9.1 Brand Ownership

- A core design/PM owner (you) acts as the final approver of any changes to:
  - Logo.
  - Core palette.
  - Typography stack.
  - Tone guidelines.

### 9.2 Change Management

- Minor UI refinements can occur sprint-by-sprint.
- Any proposal to change logo, brand colors, or typefaces requires:
  - Rationale linked to user research or product strategy.
  - Visual exploration mockups.
  - Impact assessment on existing assets.

### 9.3 Brand Debt

Just as with technical debt, track "brand debt" — ad-hoc icons, off-brand colors, inconsistent phrases.

- Maintain a simple log of inconsistencies spotted.
- Periodically schedule a "design hygiene" sprint to standardise.

***

## 10. Future Evolution

As AyushBot expands beyond a single district or even beyond India:

- Core values remain: Calm, Competent, Caring.
- Palette may gain regional variants (e.g., more earthy tones in certain contexts), but state colors and risk semantics must remain universal.
- Naming may require transliteration or adaptation; keep the root "Ayush" consistent where possible.

The brand must be steady enough to inspire confidence in health systems, yet flexible enough to adapt visually and verbally to new cultures and channels.
