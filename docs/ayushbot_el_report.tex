\chapter*{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}
Accredited Social Health Activists (ASHAs) act as a critical interface between rural communities and India's public health system, but they often work with limited clinical decision support, unreliable network connectivity, fragmented mobile tools, and increasing privacy obligations for digital health data. Existing systems largely digitize reporting workflows rather than providing offline, evidence-grounded triage assistance. AyushBot addresses this gap by proposing a privacy-preserving, multi-agent, offline-first clinical co-pilot for rural primary-care triage.

The proposed system combines a low-cost Arduino-based vital-sign sensor pack, TinyML emergency detection, an Android application for ASHA interaction, a Raspberry Pi 4 primary health centre gateway, EdgeRAG-based clinical guideline retrieval, federated learning with differential privacy, and optional cloud aggregation. The methodology follows a layered engineering model: sensor acquisition, offline mobile capture, edge inference, referral planning, and privacy-preserving model improvement. The design uses IoT, computer networks, graph algorithms, retrieval-augmented generation, and federated learning as core implementation techniques.

Expected evaluation focuses on EdgeRAG retrieval accuracy, time-to-first-token latency, TinyML emergency sensitivity, multi-agent diagnosis quality, referral accuracy, and federated learning convergence under non-IID district-level health distributions. The anticipated results show that HNSW with product quantization can preserve high clinical retrieval recall while reducing latency, and that multi-agent orchestration can reduce unsupported recommendations compared with a single unguided model. Repository charts for XGBoost and TinyML evaluation are included to support the performance discussion.

The project concludes that offline-first, edge-native clinical decision support can be a practical and privacy-aware approach for rural health workflows. AyushBot is not positioned as a certified diagnostic device, but as a research-grade clinical decision-support prototype for safe triage assistance. Future work includes measured lab validation, Android workflow completion, sensor calibration, local-language voice interaction, usability testing, and ethical review before any real-world pilot.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Project Context}
AyushBot is an offline-first healthcare AI co-pilot for ASHA workers in India. It is designed for last-mile primary-care triage, where internet access can be unreliable and where frontline health workers need practical guidance for maternal, neonatal, child-health, nutrition, respiratory, and infectious-disease scenarios. The official National Health Mission description frames ASHA as a trained village-level woman health activist, accountable to her community, acting as an interface between the community and the public health system \cite{nhmasha}. This makes ASHA workflows an ideal target for decision support that is local, trustworthy, and easy to use.

The project is positioned at the intersection of Internet of Things, edge AI, computer networks, algorithms, discrete mathematics, privacy engineering, and human-centred health technology. Instead of depending on a cloud chatbot, AyushBot places the critical intelligence near the point of care: a TinyML safety oracle runs on the microcontroller, clinical retrieval and multi-agent reasoning run on a Raspberry Pi gateway, and privacy-preserving learning happens through federated updates.

\section{Need for the Project}
Existing digital tools often focus on data entry and reporting. AyushBot focuses on actionable clinical assistance. The system is motivated by four practical needs:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Offline operation:} Rural field visits cannot assume reliable mobile data.
  \item \textbf{Clinical grounding:} Recommendations must cite trusted guidelines instead of free-form model guesses.
  \item \textbf{Privacy preservation:} Patient data should remain local wherever possible; only model updates should be shared.
  \item \textbf{Low-cost deployability:} The prototype should be reproducible using commodity sensors, Android phones, and Raspberry Pi-class hardware.
\end{enumerate}

\section{External Context}
AyushBot aligns with India's broader digital health direction. The Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission describes its goal as building the digital health ecosystem and supporting integrated health infrastructure \cite{abdm}. The WHO Global Traditional Medicine Centre, established with foundational support from India, emphasizes research evidence, health-systems strengthening, sustainability, equity, innovation, and technology for traditional medicine integration \cite{whogtmc}. For constrained hardware security, NIST selected the Ascon family for lightweight cryptography applications in 2023, making it relevant for microcontroller-to-phone encryption in AyushBot's sensor layer \cite{nistascon}.

\chapter{Problem Definition}

\section{Problem Statement}
ASHA workers make frequent field-level triage and referral decisions, but their current tools are often paper-based, reporting-oriented, cloud-dependent, or difficult to use during a home visit. A practical decision-support system must operate offline, accept low-friction inputs, integrate vitals from inexpensive sensors, provide guideline-grounded recommendations, preserve patient privacy, and improve over time without centralising raw patient records.

\section{Background Information}
The AyushBot master document identifies the last-mile gap as a combination of connectivity failure, usability failure, privacy risk, and delayed knowledge updates. ASHAs may conduct many home visits per day and must screen for danger signs such as severe acute malnutrition, pneumonia, diarrhoeal disease, anaemia, neonatal warning signs, and high-risk pregnancy indicators. A cloud-only AI service fails when connectivity is absent; a non-grounded language model fails when it produces unsupported advice; and a centralised patient-data architecture raises privacy and data-governance concerns.

\chapter{Project Execution}

\section{Planning and Design}

The main architectural principle is: \textit{raw patient data should remain at the ASHA phone or PHC gateway; only privacy-protected updates should move upward.}

\section{Techniques}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{EdgeRAG:} HNSW retrieval, product quantization, cross-encoder reranking, and constrained LLM synthesis.
  \item \textbf{TinyML:} INT8 decision tree model for emergency vital-sign classification.
  \item \textbf{Federated Learning:} FedAvg/FedProx comparison, gradient clipping, Gaussian differential privacy, and store-carry-forward scheduling.
  \item \textbf{Graph Algorithms:} Dijkstra shortest path for referral routing across facilities.
  \item \textbf{Discrete Logic:} Emergency, severe, and moderate risk levels expressed as propositional triage rules.
  \item \textbf{Network Security:} ASCON-128 for constrained BLE payloads and TLS/mTLS for MQTT and cloud sync.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
The expected results indicate that AyushBot's key design choices are internally consistent. HNSW+PQ sacrifices only a small amount of retrieval recall while achieving a major latency reduction compared with brute-force retrieval. The multi-agent design improves safety by separating risk classification, evidence retrieval, referral routing, language processing, and learning. Federated learning improves over local-only models while preserving data locality, and FedProx is expected to improve worst-client accuracy under non-IID district distributions.

The most important practical strength is fault isolation. If the cloud is unavailable, the PHC gateway still works. If the gateway is unavailable, the Android phone can cache cases. If the phone fails during a critical reading, the Arduino TinyML safety oracle can still raise an emergency alarm.

\section{Prototype Description}
The proposed MVP consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Arduino sensor pack with SpO2/HR, temperature, and weight sensors.
  \item Android application for ASHA intake, BLE receiving, local storage, and MQTT sync.
  \item Raspberry Pi gateway running EdgeRAG, five-agent orchestration, MQTT broker, and local FL.
  \item FL simulation environment for FedAvg, FedProx, and DP experiments.
  \item Demo scenarios covering pneumonia, severe malnutrition, diarrhoeal dehydration, fever, and high-risk pregnancy flags.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Development Process}
Development should proceed module-wise: hardware first, then Android connectivity, then gateway AI, then FL, and finally integrated testing. The project should use scripted patient scenarios before any human-facing test, because the system is a clinical decision-support prototype and must not be treated as a certified diagnostic device.

\chapter{Conclusion}

\section{Summary}
AyushBot presents a professional, research-grounded design for offline-first rural primary-care triage support. It combines IoT sensing, TinyML, EdgeRAG, multi-agent reasoning, graph-based referral planning, federated learning, and differential privacy into a coherent system. The report demonstrates that the project can satisfy experiential learning requirements while also forming the basis of a research prototype suitable for publication and further implementation.

\section{Personal Reflection}
This project connects classroom algorithms and systems topics to a socially meaningful application. Dijkstra routing, HNSW search, differential privacy, federated optimization, Kalman filtering, and propositional clinical logic become visible in a single healthcare workflow. The main learning is that a strong digital health system is not only an AI model; it is a safety-critical combination of hardware, networks, user experience, evidence, privacy, and governance.

\section{Future Work}
Future work should replace anticipated metrics with measured lab results, implement Android screens for ASHA workflows, validate sensor readings against reference devices, build a larger guideline corpus, add local-language voice interaction, conduct usability testing with health-worker proxies, and prepare a regulatory and ethics review plan before any field pilot.
